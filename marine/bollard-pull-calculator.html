What is Bollard Pull?

Bollard pull is the static pulling force a tug can generate when it is made fast to a fixed point, traditionally a bollard on the quay. Instead of measuring how fast a vessel moves, bollard pull measures how hard a tug can pull when forward motion is essentially prevented. That makes it a very practical rating for harbor assist, coastal towing, barge work, and offshore support because operators often need to compare towing capability rather than free-running speed. In specifications and charter discussions, bollard pull is usually shown in tonnes or US tons, with certified values coming from a controlled bollard pull test.

The number matters because engine horsepower by itself does not tell the whole story. A tug converts engine power into thrust through gears, shafts, propellers, nozzles, and the hull flow around the propulsor. The quality of that conversion determines how much of the installed power becomes useful static pull. Two tugs with similar power can therefore produce noticeably different bollard pull if one has a nozzle, larger propeller diameter, better low-speed propulsive efficiency, or a hull designed specifically for towage. A calculator like this is best used for preliminary estimation, concept comparison, and sanity checks before sea trial or certified test data are available.

How to Calculate Bollard Pull

A common preliminary estimate uses an empirical K-factor multiplied by the total installed power in kilowatts. The first step is to express engine power in kilowatts because the K-values in this calculator are based on kW input. If your engine rating is in horsepower, multiply by 0.7457 to convert from HP to kW. Next, multiply by the number of engines to find total installed power. Finally, multiply that total kW by the selected K-factor. The result is bollard pull in metric tonnes. If you are working in imperial units, you can convert the final answer to US tons by multiplying tonnes by 1.10231.

The K-factor is where engineering judgment enters the estimate. Open propeller tugs often fall around 0.011 to 0.012, while ducted or Kort-nozzle arrangements are usually higher because the nozzle improves static thrust. Azimuth thrusters can also perform well at low speed, but the exact value depends on the propulsor design, loading, and hull interaction. This is why the calculator includes both a propulsion preset and a manual override. The preset gives a sensible default for quick estimates, and the override lets you apply a yard standard, fleet average, or measured back-calculation from existing vessels.

Formulas
Total Power (kW) = Engine PowerkW × Number of Engines
Engine PowerkW = Engine PowerHP × 0.7457
Bollard Pull (tonnes) = K × Total Power (kW)
Bollard Pull (US tons) = Bollard Pull (tonnes) × 1.10231

Worked example: assume a tug has two 2000 HP main engines driving open propellers and use K = 0.0115. First convert one engine to kilowatts: 2000 × 0.7457 = 1491.4 kW. Then multiply by two engines to get 2982.8 kW total installed power. Apply the K-factor: 0.0115 × 2982.8 = 34.3 tonnes of bollard pull. To express the same result in US tons, multiply 34.3 by 1.10231 to get about 37.8 US tons. If you want an equivalent thrust value, 34.3 tonnes-force corresponds to roughly 336.4 kN.

This method is intentionally simplified. Real tug performance depends on propeller diameter, rpm, gearbox ratio, wake fraction, hull clearances, fouling, machinery condition, ambient water conditions, and whether the published engine power is continuous or maximum intermittent rating. Still, the empirical formula is useful because it captures the broad relationship between installed power and static pull in a quick, transparent way. It is a screening tool for design studies and operational planning, not a substitute for measured bollard pull certification.

Propulsion K-Factor Reference Table

The table below summarizes common preliminary K-factor ranges used for tug bollard-pull estimates. These values are not universal constants. They vary with propeller efficiency, nozzle geometry, drivetrain losses, and how well the propulsion system is optimized for static thrust rather than transit speed. Use them as starting points and refine them against known vessels whenever you can.

Propulsion Type K-Factor Range Notes
Open Propeller0.011-0.012Common baseline for conventional twin-screw tugs without nozzles; efficiency depends heavily on propeller diameter and low-speed loading.
Ducted/Kort Nozzle0.013-0.015Nozzle improves static thrust and often produces a higher bollard pull for the same installed power, especially in towing service.
Azimuth/Z-drive0.013Strong maneuverability and good thrust performance, but exact value varies with propulsor design and hull integration.
Tractor Tug0.014-0.016High-performance escort and harbor-assist designs may reach the upper end when optimized for static pull and steering force.

When selecting a K-factor, try to match not only the propulsion category but also the operational role. A harbor tug optimized for maximum pull at very low speed should not be compared directly with a multi-purpose workboat that sacrifices some static thrust for transit efficiency or deck utility. If your organization has historical test data, calibrating K against actual vessels will produce better estimates than relying on generic ranges alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is bollard pull?

Bollard pull is the static towing force a tug can produce while secured to a fixed point. It is commonly reported in metric tonnes or US tons and is one of the main performance ratings used when comparing tugs for towing or harbor-assist work.

How is bollard pull different from engine power?

Engine power describes the energy available from the machinery, while bollard pull describes the thrust that actually reaches the towline in a static condition. Propeller type, nozzle use, drivetrain losses, and hull interaction determine how effectively installed power is converted into pull.

What affects K-factor for a tug?

K-factor is influenced by propulsion arrangement, propeller diameter and pitch, gearbox ratio, nozzle geometry, hull-propeller interaction, and overall condition of the tug. It is an empirical shortcut, so its accuracy improves when you base it on similar vessels or measured trial data.

Is measured or theoretical bollard pull more accurate?

Measured bollard pull is more accurate because it comes from an actual test with calibrated instrumentation. Theoretical or empirical estimates are useful early in design, for tender screening, and for back-of-the-envelope checks, but they cannot capture every loss and operating detail.

What bollard pull rating do I need for towing?

The correct rating depends on the tow size, required control margin, environmental loads, local regulations, and the specific mission. Harbor docking, coastal towing, escort work, and offshore towing all impose different demands, so bollard pull should be selected together with maneuverability, winch capacity, and safety factors.
