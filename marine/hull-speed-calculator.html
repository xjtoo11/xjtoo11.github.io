What is Hull Speed?

Hull speed is the traditional rule-of-thumb estimate for the maximum efficient speed of a displacement boat. It is not a hard physical speed limit in the same sense as a mechanical governor, but it does mark the range where wave-making resistance begins to rise rapidly for conventional monohulls. As a displacement hull moves through the water, it creates a bow wave and a stern wave. At low and moderate speeds the boat fits comfortably between those waves, but as speed increases the wavelength grows. Eventually the distance between wave crests approaches the boat’s waterline length, and the hull starts to climb its own bow wave.

That is why waterline length matters so much. A longer boat can support a longer wave pattern and therefore reach a higher efficient speed before resistance rises sharply. Sailors, naval architects, and boat owners use hull speed to estimate cruising potential, compare designs, and understand why adding power to a heavy displacement hull may produce only a small speed gain. The classic displacement value uses a speed-length ratio of about 1.34, which works well for many traditional sailboats and full-displacement craft, while faster semi-displacement and planing boats can operate above that range.

How to Calculate Hull Speed

The classic hull speed equation is simple: multiply the speed-length ratio by the square root of waterline length. When waterline length is entered in feet, the result is in knots. For metric users, the calculator converts the knot value into kilometers per hour after performing the same underlying speed-length calculation. The default S/L ratio of 1.34 represents a common displacement-hull assumption. If you enter a higher value, you can explore semi-displacement or planing behavior; if you use a lower value, you can model a more conservative operating condition.

Because the formula is based on waterline length rather than overall length, it is important to enter the portion of the hull that is actually in contact with the water in normal loading condition. Boats with long overhangs may have a noticeably shorter waterline than their advertised overall length. Conversely, some modern designs carry waterline length far forward and aft, which increases their potential displacement speed for a given LOA. Once the speed is calculated, Froude number provides a more physics-based check. It compares boat speed with the wave speed associated with hull length and gravity, making it useful for comparing boats of different sizes on the same nondimensional basis.

Formulas
Hull Speed (knots) = S/L × √LWLft
Hull Speed (km/h) = 1.852 × Hull Speed (knots)
Froude Number = V / √(g × LWLm)

Worked example: let waterline length equal 30 ft and use the standard displacement S/L ratio of 1.34. First calculate the square root of waterline length: √30 = 5.477. Then multiply by the ratio: 1.34 × 5.477 = 7.34 knots. If you want the equivalent metric speed, multiply 7.34 by 1.852 to get 13.59 km/h. To estimate Froude number, convert 30 ft to 9.144 m and convert 7.34 knots to 3.78 m/s. Then divide 3.78 by √(9.81 × 9.144), which gives approximately 0.40.

That last value is useful because it connects the old hull-speed rule to a broader hydrodynamic framework. Displacement craft often operate in the lower Froude range, semi-displacement boats extend higher with more power and suitable hull form, and planing hulls break into a different regime altogether. The calculator therefore shows both the traditional speed result and the supporting indicators that help interpret what the number means in practice rather than treating it as an absolute cap.

S/L Ratio Reference Table

Speed-length ratio is a practical shorthand for how aggressively a boat is moving relative to its waterline length. Traditional heavy cruisers and displacement sailboats usually remain near the lower range, while lighter powerboats and advanced hull forms operate well above it. Use the table below as a quick interpretation guide rather than a strict classification rule, since actual behavior also depends on displacement, trim, hull slenderness, and available power.

In day-to-day use, the table is most helpful when you are setting expectations rather than predicting exact performance. A cruising sailboat operating around 1.2 to 1.34 S/L is typically near its comfortable displacement limit, while a patrol craft or fast trawler may be designed to spend long periods in the semi-displacement range with enough installed power to push past the classic bow-wave hump. Once S/L rises well above 2.5, the boat is generally relying on dynamic lift, trim control, and hull geometry in ways that the simple displacement formula alone cannot fully describe.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is hull speed for a boat?

Hull speed is the traditional theoretical maximum efficient speed for a displacement boat. It is estimated from waterline length because longer boats generate longer wave patterns and can travel faster before wave-making resistance rises sharply.

Can a boat go faster than hull speed?

Yes. Hull speed is a rule-of-thumb for displacement behavior, not an absolute barrier. Boats can exceed it if they have enough power, a light enough displacement, a slender enough hull, or a hull shape capable of semi-planing or planing operation.

Why does waterline length matter more than overall length?

Waterline length is what interacts directly with the water to create the dominant wave system. Overall length may include bow or stern overhangs that add little to displacement-wave behavior, so LWL is the more useful number for hull speed estimates.

What is the theoretical basis for hull speed?

The concept comes from wave-making resistance. As speed increases, the wavelength associated with the hull’s wake increases until it becomes comparable to the boat’s waterline length. Froude number expresses that same relationship in a dimensionless hydrodynamic form.

How does hull shape affect hull speed?

Hull shape affects drag, trim, wake formation, and the boat’s ability to transition into higher-speed regimes. A narrow lightweight hull may exceed classic displacement limits more easily than a broad heavy hull with similar waterline length.
