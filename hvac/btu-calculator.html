What is a BTU?

BTU stands for British Thermal Unit. It is a unit of heat energy defined as the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of one pound of water by one degree Fahrenheit. In HVAC work, the term is usually used as BTU per hour, written BTU/h, because heating and cooling equipment moves heat continuously over time rather than in a single lump of energy. A small room air conditioner, a split system, and a furnace are all rated by how many BTU per hour they can remove or add.

That number matters because HVAC sizing is really a heat transfer problem. If a room gains 14,000 BTU of heat each hour on a hot day, the cooling equipment has to remove about the same amount to hold temperature. If the unit is too small, the space may never reach setpoint. If it is too large, it may cool quickly but run in short bursts, which hurts humidity control and comfort. Knowing the required BTU load gives you a better starting point for selecting AC tonnage, checking rule-of-thumb estimates, and deciding when you need a more detailed Manual J calculation.

How to Calculate BTU for HVAC

A useful quick HVAC BTU estimate starts with floor area, because larger rooms generally have more wall area, more air volume, and more surface through which heat can enter or leave. Climate comes next. A 500 sq ft room in a hot, humid market needs a different base load than the same room in a cool northern climate, so this calculator assigns a climate-zone BTU-per-square-foot factor before any other adjustments are applied.

From there, the load is adjusted for insulation quality, sun exposure, ceiling height, windows, and occupants. Poor insulation increases the load because more heat enters through the envelope. Good insulation reduces it. Higher sun exposure increases solar gain through walls and especially glazing. Ceiling height matters because a room with a 10-foot ceiling contains more conditioned volume than one with a standard 8-foot ceiling. Windows add a separate sensible load because glass is typically weaker than insulated walls and often admits direct solar energy. Occupants matter too: people release sensible heat, and in small rooms or crowded spaces that load is not trivial.

This page uses a simplified engineering formula suitable for landing-page estimating, not final design. It intentionally avoids the detail of a full Manual J, but it still captures the biggest load drivers that break simple square-foot rules.

Simplified Formula
Base Load = Area × Base Climate BTU per sq ft
Ceiling Adjustment = 1 + (Ceiling Height - 8) × 0.02, for ceilings above 8 ft
Window Load = Window Area × 50
Occupant Load = Occupants × 600
Total BTU/h = (Base Load × Insulation Multiplier × Sun Multiplier × Ceiling Adjustment) + Window Load + Occupant Load
AC Tonnage = Total BTU/h / 12,000

Worked example using the default values in this calculator: room area is 500 sq ft, ceiling height is 8 ft, insulation is Average, window area is 40 sq ft, climate zone is 4, sun exposure is Medium, and occupants are 2. Zone 4 uses a base factor of 26 BTU per sq ft, so the base load is 500 × 26 = 13,000 BTU/h. Average insulation uses a multiplier of 1.0, and Medium sun also uses 1.0, so the adjusted base remains 13,000 BTU/h. The ceiling is 8 ft, so there is no ceiling premium and the ceiling adjustment stays at 1.00.

Next add window and occupant loads. Window load is 40 × 50 = 2,000 BTU/h. Occupant load is 2 × 600 = 1,200 BTU/h. Total BTU/h is 13,000 + 2,000 + 1,200 = 16,200 BTU/h. Recommended AC tonnage is 16,200 / 12,000 = 1.35 tons. BTU per square foot is 16,200 / 500 = 32.4 BTU/sq ft. That is higher than the base climate factor because the windows and occupants add load on top of the envelope estimate.

Field tips: do not ignore kitchen appliances, bathrooms with exhaust-driven infiltration, or ductwork running through hot attics. If you are estimating several connected rooms, calculate each one and then evaluate diversity and common solar exposure instead of blindly combining square footage. Oversizing is not a harmless safety margin. An oversized AC can short cycle, dehumidify poorly, and create cold blasts followed by warm swings. Use quick BTU tools for screening, then move to a room-by-room load method when the project cost or comfort risk justifies it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a BTU in HVAC?

A BTU is a unit of heat energy. In HVAC, BTU per hour is used to describe how much heating or cooling capacity equipment can deliver. When someone says an air conditioner is 24,000 BTU, they usually mean 24,000 BTU per hour of cooling capacity.

How many BTU per square foot do I need?

Many quick estimates fall between 18 and 30 BTU per square foot, but that range changes fast with climate, ceiling height, window area, sun exposure, and insulation. Kitchens, sunrooms, and server rooms can exceed normal residential rules by a wide margin.

What is the difference between BTU and tons?

BTU per hour is the direct heat transfer rate. Tons are a larger AC sizing unit where 1 ton equals 12,000 BTU/h. Converting between them is simple: divide BTU/h by 12,000 to estimate required tonnage.

Can I use this calculator for heating?

You can use it for rough screening, but heating loads are driven by winter design temperatures, infiltration, and envelope losses in ways that differ from summer cooling. For furnace or cold-climate heat pump selection, use a dedicated heating load or full Manual J calculation.

What happens if my AC unit is too big?

An oversized AC often satisfies thermostat temperature too quickly, then shuts off before it removes enough moisture. That short cycling can leave the home cool but clammy, increase start-stop wear, and reduce overall comfort compared with a properly sized unit.
