What is Glycol and Why is it Used in HVAC?

Glycol is an antifreeze additive mixed with water to protect HVAC piping and coils from freezing. You see it most often in hydronic heating loops, chilled water systems exposed to low ambient conditions, snow melt piping, and ground-source heat pump circuits. Plain water transfers heat well and is easy to pump, but once fluid temperature can drop near 32°F, freeze damage becomes a real design and maintenance risk. A glycol-water mixture lowers the solution freeze point and provides a wider safety margin during shutdowns, low-load operation, winter outages, or control failures.

In practice, the two common options are ethylene glycol and propylene glycol. Ethylene glycol usually gives better thermal performance at the same concentration because it tends to have slightly better heat transfer characteristics and lower viscosity than propylene glycol. The tradeoff is toxicity, which limits where it can be used. Propylene glycol is less toxic and is commonly chosen where food processing, occupied spaces, or potable-water-adjacent equipment make safety the higher priority. Neither fluid should be treated as just generic antifreeze. The inhibitor package, compatibility with metals and seals, and the required freeze protection level all matter. The right glycol choice is therefore a balance between freeze protection, pumping penalty, maintenance chemistry, and the specific safety requirements of the system.

How to Calculate Glycol Freeze Point

The key input is concentration. As glycol concentration rises, the solution can withstand lower temperatures before ice crystals begin to form. That sounds simple, but the relationship is not linear. A jump from 10% to 20% concentration does not produce the same temperature change as a jump from 40% to 50%. That is why field calculators and manufacturer charts usually rely on tested tables instead of a single straight-line formula. This calculator uses lookup tables for ethylene and propylene glycol, then interpolates between concentration points to estimate freeze point, burst point, specific gravity, and viscosity increase factor.

Freeze point and burst point are related but not identical. Freeze point is the temperature where the first slush or ice crystals form in the fluid. At that point, the solution may still flow in some conditions, but heat transfer and pumpability are already compromised. Burst point is lower. It represents the temperature where the expanding slush can generate enough pressure to crack a coil, pipe, or heat exchanger if the system is static or trapped. In design terms, freeze point is an operating-performance concern and burst point is a damage-risk concern. Engineers usually choose a concentration that keeps the freeze point safely below the expected minimum fluid temperature, not just above the burst point.

Mixing Formulas
Glycol Volume (gal) = System Volume (gal) × Concentration (%) / 100
Water Volume (gal) = System Volume (gal) − Glycol Volume (gal)

Worked example: a 100 gallon system using 30% ethylene glycol needs 30 gallons of glycol and 70 gallons of water. From the reference data, the freeze point is 3°F and the burst point is -15°F. The specific gravity is 1.039 and the viscosity increase factor is 1.5x compared with water. Practically, that means the loop has meaningful freeze protection for a light winter exposure, but it also means the pumps and heat exchangers no longer behave like a pure-water system. The fluid is denser, the pump sees higher friction loss, and heat transfer drops slightly. Those effects are manageable at 30%, but they get more severe as concentration climbs.

There are a few field rules worth keeping in mind. First, verify concentration with a refractometer rather than guessing from what was originally added. Top-offs, leaks, and partial drains can change the actual mix. Second, avoid pushing concentration above about 60% unless the application truly requires it. Higher percentages usually bring diminishing returns in freeze protection while making viscosity and pump penalty much worse. Third, use propylene glycol when the system is near potable water or food-service spaces and safety drives the selection. Fourth, check fluid pH and inhibitor condition annually, because the chemistry package is just as important as the freeze point. Finally, use inhibited HVAC-grade glycol rather than automotive coolant. The corrosion package and compatibility targets are different, and the wrong product can damage the system even if the freeze point looks acceptable.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between ethylene glycol and propylene glycol?

Ethylene glycol typically gives better heat transfer and somewhat lower viscosity at the same freeze-protection target, which is why it is common in closed industrial and commercial loops. Its drawback is toxicity. Propylene glycol is less toxic and is usually preferred in food plants, ice rinks, schools, and potable-water-adjacent systems. The safer fluid often comes with a larger pumping penalty, so the choice is usually a balance between safety requirements and hydraulic performance.

What glycol concentration should I use?

Select concentration from the minimum expected fluid temperature, then add a reasonable design margin. Many HVAC systems end up between 25% and 40%, but outdoor piping, heat recovery loops, or idle equipment in cold climates may need more. Avoid blindly maximizing concentration. Once you get into very strong mixtures, viscosity rises quickly and the extra freeze protection often comes at the cost of worse heat transfer and higher pump horsepower.

What is the difference between freeze point and burst point?

Freeze point is when crystals first appear in the solution. The fluid may become slushy and lose performance, but the piping may still survive if temperatures do not keep dropping. Burst point is lower and relates to the temperature where expansion of the partially frozen mixture can break a closed pipe or coil. Designers care about both values, but for reliable operation they usually size concentration to stay safely above the freeze point margin.

How do I measure glycol concentration in the field?

The most common field tool is a refractometer calibrated for the glycol type being tested. Take a representative sample, confirm the scale matches ethylene or propylene glycol, and compare the reading to the manufacturer chart or a trusted calculator. Hydrometers can also be used in some programs, but refractometers are generally faster and less sensitive to operator error. Do not assume color tells you anything useful about concentration or inhibitor condition.

How often should I replace glycol in an HVAC system?

Replacement depends on fluid condition, not just elapsed years. Test concentration, pH, reserve alkalinity, inhibitor condition, and contamination at regular intervals, commonly once a year. If the fluid has degraded, lost inhibitor protection, or picked up significant contaminants, it may need re-inhibition or replacement. A loop that still tests within spec can often stay in service much longer than one that has been overheated, diluted, or exposed to oxygen ingress.
