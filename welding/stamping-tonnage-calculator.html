What is Stamping Tonnage?

Stamping tonnage is the amount of force a press must deliver to cut or form sheet material during a stamping operation. In blanking and piercing, tonnage is the shearing force needed to push the punch through the workpiece and separate the metal cleanly along the cutting edge. That force depends on three main inputs: the total cut perimeter, the material thickness, and the material shear strength. A larger perimeter means more edge length is being cut at once. A thicker sheet increases the area being sheared. A stronger alloy resists the cut more heavily, so the press has to work harder.

Understanding tonnage is essential for press selection, die design, scheduling, and safe production planning. If the calculated requirement is too low, the press may overload, punches can chip, and edge quality may suffer. If the estimate is too conservative, the job may be assigned to a larger and more expensive machine than necessary. That is why engineers usually begin with the theoretical shearing force and then apply a practical safety factor. The result is a realistic press tonnage requirement that supports consistent blanking and piercing without overstressing equipment. Even though the equation is simple, using the right units and material data is what makes it valuable on the shop floor.

How to Calculate Press Tonnage

For blanking and piercing, press tonnage is calculated from the total cut perimeter P, material thickness T, and the material shear strength S. The basic idea is straightforward: the press must shear the full perimeter of the part through the full thickness of the sheet. The product of those three values gives the base cutting force. From there, a safety factor is applied to reflect real operating conditions such as punch wear, die clearance variation, inconsistent stock properties, and impact loading near breakthrough.

In imperial units, perimeter is entered in inches, thickness in inches, and shear strength in psi. In practice, many tables list shear strength in ksi, so the calculator converts ksi to psi internally by multiplying by 1,000. The base force is produced in pounds-force, and the required press tonnage is then found by dividing by 2,000 pounds per ton and multiplying by the safety factor. In metric units, perimeter is entered in millimeters, thickness in millimeters, and shear strength in MPa. Since 1 MPa equals 1 N/mm², the same multiplication directly gives force in newtons. Dividing by 1,000 converts the result to kN before the safety factor is applied.

Formulas
Force (lbs) = P × T × S, where S is in psi
Required Tonnage (tons) = Force ÷ 2000 × Safety Factor
Force (N) = P × T × S, where S is in Pa
Required Force (kN) = Force ÷ 1000 × Safety Factor

Worked example: use a cut perimeter of 12 in, material thickness of 0.1 in, Mild Steel 1018 with shear strength of 45 ksi, and a safety factor of 1.2. Convert 45 ksi to 45,000 psi. The cutting force becomes 12 × 0.1 × 45,000 = 54,000 lbs. Then divide by 2,000 to convert to tons: 54,000 ÷ 2,000 = 27 tons. After multiplying by the 1.2 safety factor, the required press tonnage is 32.4 tons. This is the default example shown in the calculator. The same logic applies to piercing multiple holes or blanking larger profiles: just use the total active cut perimeter for the stroke and choose a safety factor consistent with your tooling and production risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is stamping tonnage?

Stamping tonnage is the force required for a press to perform a cutting or forming operation. For blanking and piercing, it is based on cut perimeter, sheet thickness, and the material's shear strength, then adjusted with a safety factor for real production use.

Why include a safety factor for press tonnage?

A safety factor covers the gap between an ideal calculation and actual shop conditions. Material properties vary, tools wear, clearances drift, and breakthrough loads can shock the press. A modest factor helps keep the selected machine out of overload territory.

How does material thickness affect tonnage?

Tonnage increases directly with thickness. If the cut perimeter and material strength stay the same, doubling the sheet thickness doubles the force needed to shear the part. Thickness is therefore one of the fastest ways to drive press demand upward.

Blanking vs piercing tonnage — any difference?

The same basic equation is used for both because each operation shears material along a cutting edge. In practice, the total active perimeter, stripping force, number of punches, and desired edge condition may change how the die is designed, but the core cutting force calculation is the same.

What happens if press tonnage is underestimated?

An undersized press can overload mechanically, produce rough or incomplete cuts, accelerate punch and die damage, and create serious safety issues. It also reduces process reliability because the press may struggle to maintain repeatable performance from stroke to stroke.
