What is K-Factor?

K-factor is a practical sheet metal forming value that describes where the neutral axis sits through the thickness of a part while it is bent. During bending, the inside surface is compressed and the outside surface is stretched. Between those layers is the neutral axis, the zone that changes length the least. K-factor expresses the neutral axis position as a fraction of material thickness measured from the inside face. If the neutral axis is exactly halfway through the thickness, the K-factor would be 0.50. In real brake forming, it is usually lower because the neutral axis shifts inward toward the compressed side.

This value matters because bend allowance depends on the neutral axis location, not simply on the inside or outside surface. If your K-factor estimate is wrong, the flat pattern will also be wrong, and that can lead to bad flange lengths, hole position drift, assembly fit problems, and extra press brake setup time. K-factor is therefore one of the most useful shortcut values in fabrication planning. Engineers use it during design, estimators use it while quoting formed parts, and brake operators use it to tune flat blanks to real tooling behavior. It is not a universal constant for a material. Instead, it changes with thickness, bend radius, temper, tooling, and bending method, so calculators provide a strong starting point that shops can refine with test bends.

How to Calculate K-Factor

A fully empirical K-factor comes from shop testing, but a useful first-pass estimate can be built from material type and the ratio of inside bend radius to material thickness. That ratio, written as R/T, captures how severe the bend is. Tight bends with a small radius compared with thickness tend to pull the neutral axis inward and produce lower K-factors. Larger radii move the neutral axis outward and generally increase the value. Material softness matters too. Softer alloys and tempers usually bend more easily and often start from a lower base value, while harder materials and stainless grades tend to run higher.

This calculator uses a simplified empirical model. Each material family starts with a base K-factor, then a logarithmic adjustment is applied using the R/T ratio. The logarithmic term keeps the change moderate: it raises K-factor gradually as radius grows instead of allowing unrealistic jumps. The result is clamped to a practical range of 0.25 to 0.50 because most sheet metal work falls inside that band. Once K-factor is known, the neutral axis location from the inside face is simply K multiplied by thickness.

Formulas
R/T Ratio = R ÷ T
K = clamp(Base K + log10(max(R/T, 0.1)) × 0.05, 0.25, 0.50)
Neutral Axis Location = K × T

Worked example: use material thickness T = 2 mm, inside bend radius R = 3 mm, and material type Mild Steel. First calculate the ratio R/T = 3/2 = 1.5. Mild steel uses a base K-factor of 0.40. The logarithmic term is log10(1.5), which is about 0.176. Multiply that by 0.05 and the adjustment becomes 0.0088. Add the adjustment to the base value and you get K = 0.4088, which rounds to 0.409. The neutral axis location is then 0.409 multiplied by 2 mm, or 0.818 mm from the inside face.

That result is useful immediately in bend allowance calculations because it tells you where the neutral axis runs through the bend. In production, the best workflow is to start with an estimated K-factor, form a test coupon using real tooling, compare predicted and measured dimensions, and then update your process table if needed. For quoting, CAD flat patterns, and first-pass brake setup, this type of model is fast and usually close enough to avoid obvious mistakes. For critical parts, aerospace work, cosmetic stainless, or heavy-gauge forming, a shop-specific bend table remains the more defensible standard.

These values are intentionally conservative. Material specification alone does not lock in the true K-factor because temper, yield strength, rolling direction, lubrication, bottoming versus air bending, and even operator setup choices all influence the final position of the neutral axis. A stainless sheet bent with a narrow V-die may behave differently from the same grade bent over a larger opening. Likewise, aluminum 6061 can show noticeably different forming behavior depending on temper and whether the bend line runs with or across the grain. If you are building a production bend chart, treat reference tables as seed data rather than as final truth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is K-factor in sheet metal?

K-factor is the ratio that shows the neutral axis location within sheet thickness during bending, measured from the inside surface. It lets you convert a real bend into a usable flat-pattern calculation.

How is K-factor related to bend allowance?

K-factor appears directly in the bend allowance equation through the term R + K × T. As K-factor increases, the effective neutral-axis radius increases, which usually increases bend allowance and the developed flat length assigned to the bend.

What is a typical K-factor value?

Many common sheet metal jobs fall between about 0.30 and 0.50. Tight bends in soft material often sit near the low end, while larger-radius bends and harder materials such as stainless tend to run closer to 0.43 to 0.48.

Does K-factor change with bend angle?

The main drivers are usually radius-to-thickness ratio, material properties, and tooling rather than angle alone. However, if angle changes also alter springback, penetration, or forming method, the effective K-factor observed in production can shift.

How do I measure K-factor empirically?

Cut a test blank, bend it using the same press brake setup planned for production, measure the finished geometry, and compare it with your predicted flat pattern. Then adjust the K-factor until the calculated bend allowance matches the real part dimensions.
