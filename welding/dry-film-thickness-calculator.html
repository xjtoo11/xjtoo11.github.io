What is Dry Film Thickness?

Dry film thickness, usually shortened to DFT, is the thickness of the coating that remains on a surface after the liquid paint or lining has dried and cured. During application, a coating contains solids plus solvent or other volatile components. The wet film thickness you spray, brush, or roll onto steel is therefore not the same as the final protective layer left in service. Once the volatile portion flashes off, only the solids remain, and that remaining thickness is the value inspectors, engineers, and owners care about.

DFT matters because coating performance is strongly tied to final thickness. If the film is too thin, the coating may not provide the intended barrier against moisture, oxygen, chemicals, or abrasion. If it is too thick, the system can suffer from cracking, solvent entrapment, cure problems, or poor intercoat adhesion. Specifications for tanks, structural steel, pipelines, marine assets, and industrial equipment are therefore usually written around target DFT ranges for each coat and for the total system. Understanding DFT also helps with estimating material usage, checking whether a chosen wet film thickness will meet the specification, and planning inspection points during painting work.

How to Calculate DFT

The basic idea is simple: dry film thickness depends on how much of the wet coating volume remains after drying. That retained fraction is the volume solids percentage. If a coating has 50 percent volume solids, roughly half of the wet film remains as dry film under ideal conditions. If thinner is added, the effective solids percentage drops because you increase liquid volume without increasing the amount of solid resin and pigment left behind. That is why the first calculation is the adjusted or effective volume solids.

To calculate DFT, start with the measured or target wet film thickness and multiply it by the effective volume solids fraction. Keep units consistent. If wet film thickness is in mils, the result comes out in mils. If the input is in microns, the output comes out in microns. Coverage rate can then be estimated from the resulting DFT. The coverage value shown here is theoretical, meaning it assumes 100 percent transfer efficiency and does not account for overspray, surface roughness, container loss, or practical wastage in the field.

Formulas
Effective VS% = VS% ÷ (1 + Thinner% ÷ 100)
DFT = WFT × Effective VS% ÷ 100
Coverage (sq ft/gal) = 1604 × Effective VS fraction ÷ DFT in mils
Coverage (m2/L) = 1000 × Effective VS fraction ÷ DFT in microns

Worked example: use a wet film thickness of 6 mils, volume solids of 50 percent, and no thinner added. Effective volume solids remains 50 percent. DFT therefore equals 6 × 50 ÷ 100 = 3 mils. The theoretical coverage rate is 1604 × 0.50 ÷ 3, which gives 267 square feet per gallon. That is the same result the calculator shows with its default values. If you added 10 percent thinner to the same coating, the effective solids would drop to about 45.5 percent, the dry film thickness would fall to about 2.73 mils at the same wet application thickness, and the theoretical coverage at that thinner-adjusted film would also change. This is why painters and inspectors track both wet application thickness and mix ratio rather than relying on nominal product data alone.

In practice, treat the math as a planning tool rather than a substitute for inspection. Product data sheets can list a range of volume solids, field thinning may be limited by the manufacturer, and surface profile can absorb part of the first coat. WFT combs help applicators check the wet film immediately after spraying, while dry film gauges confirm whether the cured system actually meets the specified range. Good coating control combines both steps: calculate the target, apply to that target, and then verify the result after cure.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is dry film thickness?

Dry film thickness is the cured thickness of the coating left on the substrate after solvents evaporate and the film sets. It is the thickness that governs corrosion protection, appearance, and long-term coating performance.

What is volume solids in paint?

Volume solids is the percentage of the applied wet coating volume that remains on the surface as dry solid material. Higher volume solids generally produce a higher DFT at the same wet film thickness.

How accurate is a calculated DFT?

The calculation is a theoretical estimate based on stated solids content and uniform application. Real results vary with thinner addition, spray efficiency, surface profile, environmental conditions, and product-specific behavior, so field measurement is still required.

What tools measure DFT in the field?

Inspectors usually measure cured DFT with magnetic or electronic coating thickness gauges. During application they may also use a wet film thickness comb to estimate what the final DFT should be after drying.

What happens if DFT is too thin or too thick?

Too little dry film can leave the substrate underprotected and shorten service life. Too much can cause cracking, poor cure, solvent entrapment, reduced adhesion, or topcoat problems depending on the coating chemistry and service conditions.
