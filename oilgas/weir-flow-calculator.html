What is a Weir?

A weir is a simple open-channel flow measuring device built around a shaped crest or opening that forces liquid to pass through a known geometry. Once the crest is installed level and the upstream flow settles, the head above the crest becomes a practical indicator of discharge. Engineers use that relationship in irrigation channels, produced-water systems, treatment plants, mine dewatering systems, and field test setups because it converts a difficult flow measurement problem into a direct level measurement problem.

The most common sharp-crested types are V-notch and rectangular weirs. A V-notch weir narrows the opening to a triangular notch, which makes small changes in low flow easier to detect because discharge rises with head to the 2.5 power. Rectangular weirs use a straight crest and are often chosen when flows are larger or when a broader opening is easier to fabricate and maintain. Contracted rectangular weirs allow side contractions, while suppressed rectangular weirs span the full channel width so the side contractions are removed from the standard field equation.

Although a weir is mechanically simple, accuracy depends on installation quality. The crest must be sharp, clean, and fully aerated. The upstream head must be measured at a proper distance from the plate, not right at the nappe where drawdown occurs. Submergence, debris, leakage, sediment, and approach velocity effects can all distort the result if they are ignored.

How to Calculate Weir Flow

Weir flow calculations begin with one field measurement: the head H above the crest. That head is measured vertically from the crest elevation to the upstream water surface at a point far enough upstream that the local surface is not pulled down by acceleration over the opening. Once H is known, the correct equation depends on the geometry. A V-notch weir uses the notch angle, while a rectangular weir uses the crest width. In practice, most handbooks publish simple Imperial equations for routine field use and more general coefficient-based equations for Metric work and calibration-based analysis.

For Imperial calculations in this calculator, the standard empirical forms are used directly. A fully contracted V-notch follows Q = 2.49 tan(θ/2) H2.5, with H in feet and Q in cubic feet per second. A suppressed rectangular weir follows Francis’ equation Q = 3.33 L H1.5. A contracted rectangular weir uses Q = 3.33 (L - 0.2H) H1.5, which reduces the effective crest width to account for end contractions. In those field equations, the empirical coefficient is already embedded in the constant, so the user-entered discharge coefficient is mainly relevant when switching to the Metric theoretical form.

For Metric calculations, this page uses the coefficient-based sharp-crested equations with g = 9.81 m/s2. The V-notch form is Q = (8/15) Cd √(2g) tan(θ/2) H2.5. The rectangular form is Q = (2/3) Cd √(2g) L H1.5. Those expressions make the role of Cd explicit, which is useful when test data, standards, or site calibration require an adjusted coefficient rather than a built-in empirical constant. If you do not have a calibrated value, use the coefficient conservatively and confirm it against the governing standard for your installation.

Worked example: for a 90 degree V-notch with head H = 0.5 ft, first evaluate the angle term. Because θ/2 = 45 degrees, tan(45 degrees) = 1. Next evaluate the head term: 0.52.5 = 0.1768. Multiply through: Q = 2.49 × 1 × 0.1768 = 0.44 cfs. Converting to gallons per minute gives about 198 GPM. That is the default example shown in the calculator and is a useful reference point for checking the math in the field.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a weir used for?

A weir is used to measure or regulate open-channel flow. By forcing liquid over a known crest shape and measuring the upstream head, engineers can estimate discharge without inserting a mechanical flowmeter into the stream.

When should I use a V-notch vs rectangular weir?

Use a V-notch when the expected flow is low or when you need better sensitivity to small changes in head. Use a rectangular weir when the flow is higher, when a wider opening is practical, or when the channel geometry suits a straight crest more naturally.

What is the discharge coefficient?

The discharge coefficient Cd corrects the ideal theoretical equation so it matches real flow. It captures effects from crest sharpness, contraction, aeration, viscosity, and approach conditions, and it is often set from calibration data or published standards.

How accurate is weir flow measurement?

Weirs can be very accurate, but only when installation and operation are controlled. Poor head measurement, submergence, debris, sediment deposition, leakage around the plate, and an unventilated nappe can introduce large errors even if the formula itself is correct.

What are the installation requirements for a weir?

The plate should be level, plumb, sharp-crested, and sealed against leakage. The upstream section should be straight and calm, the head gauge should be located upstream away from drawdown, and the downstream side should allow free, aerated discharge rather than drowned flow.
