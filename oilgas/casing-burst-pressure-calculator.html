What is Casing Burst Pressure?

Casing burst pressure is the internal pressure at which the casing wall is expected to reach yield in hoop stress. In oil and gas well design, this is one of the basic pressure-containment checks used to confirm that production casing, intermediate casing, or liners can tolerate expected internal loads during drilling, cementing, stimulation, production, shut-in, and well-control events. The calculation is not the whole design basis by itself, but it is a fast first-pass screen that helps engineers compare sizes, weights, and grades before running a more complete design review.

The most common hand calculation uses Barlow’s formula. It relates burst capacity to outside diameter, wall thickness, and material yield strength. For API 5CT casing, engineers often include a wall tolerance factor of 0.875 so the calculation reflects a conservative portion of nominal wall thickness rather than assuming the pipe is exactly at catalog thickness everywhere around the circumference. That makes the result more practical for preliminary sizing, field checks, and quick engineering estimates.

Burst pressure should not be confused with allowable working pressure. Real design must also consider connection efficiency, temperature effects, corrosion allowance, wear, collapse, axial load, biaxial or triaxial stress interaction, and company safety policy. Still, the burst estimate remains a useful and widely recognized starting point because it shows how much hoop-stress resistance the selected casing body can theoretically provide.

How to Calculate Burst Pressure

The API-style burst estimate for casing starts with Barlow’s formula. In its practical oilfield form, the equation multiplies yield strength by twice the wall thickness, applies the API wall tolerance factor of 0.875, and divides by outside diameter. The result is the internal pressure that would bring the pipe body to yield in hoop stress based on nominal geometry and specified minimum yield strength. Higher strength grades increase burst pressure directly, while larger diameter lowers it if wall thickness and grade stay the same.

Because the equation is ratio-based, unit consistency is all that matters. If you enter OD and wall thickness in inches and yield strength in psi, the burst result comes out in psi. If you switch to millimeters and MPa, the same formula still works and returns MPa. That makes the calculator easy to use across field reports, tubing and casing data sheets, and international design workflows.

D over t is also important because geometry changes how the pipe behaves under pressure. A low D/t ratio means a relatively thick wall for the size, which generally improves structural resistance. A high D/t ratio indicates a thinner wall section and makes the pipe more sensitive to collapse and instability effects. That is why the calculator reports D/t next to burst pressure instead of treating pressure capacity as only a material-strength problem.

Formula
Pb = (2 × Y × t × 0.875) / OD
D/t = OD / t

Worked example: use OD = 9.625 in, wall thickness t = 0.545 in, and API 5CT grade N-80 with minimum yield strength Y = 80,000 psi. First multiply the material and wall terms: 2 × 80,000 × 0.545 = 87,200. Then apply the wall tolerance factor: 87,200 × 0.875 = 76,300. Finally divide by outside diameter: 76,300 / 9.625 = about 7,930 psi. Using the exact decimals shown in the calculator gives about 7,927 psi, which is the same result with normal rounding differences.

The page also shows an approximate collapse pressure as a secondary indicator. That number uses a simplified elastic-shell estimate, not the full API collapse equations. It is useful for quick comparison only and should not be treated as an API-rated collapse value for well design. For casing programs, final selection should always be checked against the applicable collapse regime, connection limits, burst-load case, and any load interaction rules required by the operator or governing standard.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is casing burst pressure?

Casing burst pressure is the internal pressure level that causes the casing body to reach yield in hoop stress. It is a pipe-body pressure rating estimate, not automatically the allowable operating pressure for the full string.

Why 0.875 in the Barlow formula?

The 0.875 factor is a conservative API wall tolerance allowance. It reduces the effective contribution of nominal wall thickness so the burst estimate reflects manufacturing tolerance instead of assuming the pipe wall is at nominal thickness everywhere.

What is the difference between burst and collapse pressure?

Burst pressure is resistance to internal pressure pushing the pipe outward. Collapse pressure is resistance to external pressure squeezing the pipe inward. Burst is largely a hoop-yield problem, while collapse is strongly influenced by D/t ratio, ovality, residual stress, and collapse regime.

Which API 5CT grade should I choose?

Choose the grade that satisfies burst, collapse, tensile, environment, and cost requirements for the actual load cases in your well. N-80 is common for stronger general service, while L-80, C-90, and T-95 are often considered when sour-service or corrosion constraints matter.

How much safety factor should I apply?

There is no single universal factor. Operators usually set design factors based on company standards, uncertainty in predicted loads, and the consequence of failure. Theoretical burst pressure should be treated as a capacity estimate, then reduced by the required design margin for actual operation.
