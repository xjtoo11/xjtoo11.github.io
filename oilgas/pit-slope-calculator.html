What is Pit Slope Stability?

Pit slope stability describes how safely the walls of an open pit mine can stand at a given height and angle without sliding or collapsing along a potential failure surface. In day-to-day mine planning, slope stability affects stripping ratio, ore access, haul-road placement, catch-bench width, and the overall economics of the excavation. A steeper wall usually reduces waste removal and can improve project value, but every increase in angle also increases the driving forces that push rock or soil downslope. The geotechnical engineer therefore has to balance production goals against an acceptable level of risk.

The behavior of a slope depends on material strength, groundwater conditions, discontinuities, blasting disturbance, weathering, and geometry. Strong dry rock with favorable structure can support much steeper walls than weak saturated material. Even in competent rock, however, water pressure and adverse joints can sharply reduce the available shear resistance. Because of that, mine slopes are usually assessed with a combination of field mapping, laboratory testing, hydrogeology, and engineering analysis rather than by geometry alone.

This calculator uses a simplified infinite-slope style equation to give a quick screening estimate of factor of safety. It is useful for comparing scenarios early in planning, checking sensitivity to pore pressure, and understanding how cohesion and friction interact. It is not a substitute for a detailed design study, but it helps translate basic geotechnical inputs into an understandable stability metric.

How to Calculate Factor of Safety

In slope engineering, factor of safety is the ratio of resisting shear strength to driving shear stress along a possible sliding surface. If the resisting forces equal the driving forces, the factor of safety is 1.0. Values above 1.0 indicate some reserve capacity, while values below 1.0 imply that the slope would be expected to fail under the assumptions used in the model. For mine planning, engineers often prefer a factor of safety comfortably above unity because real slopes contain uncertainty in strength, water conditions, and geologic structure.

The simplified method on this page starts by estimating the total normal stress on a plane parallel to the slope using the unit weight of the material, the slope height, and the cosine of the slope angle. Driving shear stress is estimated from the same unit weight and height terms, multiplied by the sine and cosine of the slope angle. Shear resistance is then taken as the sum of cohesion plus the frictional component derived from effective normal stress. Pore pressure enters through the pore pressure ratio ru, which reduces the effective normal stress available for friction. As ru rises, the frictional resistance falls and the factor of safety drops.

This approach is intentionally compact. It does not model circular failure surfaces explicitly, bench geometry, anisotropy, structural wedges, or nonuniform groundwater. Still, it captures the first-order relationships that matter most in a screening check: taller and steeper slopes tend to be less stable, while higher cohesion and friction angle tend to improve stability. That makes it useful for quick tradeoff studies when you want to understand the direction and rough magnitude of a design change before moving to full limit-equilibrium software.

Formula
σ = γ × H × cos2(β)
τdriving = γ × H × sin(β) × cos(β)
τresisting = c + σ × (1 - ru) × tan(φ)
FOS = τresisting / τdriving

Worked example: use H = 150 ft, β = 45 degrees, c = 1,500 psf, φ = 35 degrees, γ = 165 pcf, and ru = 0.1. First compute normal stress: σ = 165 × 150 × cos2(45 degrees). Since cos2(45 degrees) = 0.5, σ = 12,375 psf. Next compute driving shear stress: τdriving = 165 × 150 × sin(45 degrees) × cos(45 degrees) = 12,375 psf. Then reduce the normal stress for pore pressure and apply friction: τresisting = 1,500 + 12,375 × 0.9 × tan(35 degrees), which is about 1,500 + 7,795 = 9,295 psf. Finally divide resisting by driving stress: FOS = 9,295 / 12,375 = 0.75. Under this simplified check, the slope is unstable, which tells the planner that the angle is too aggressive unless the material properties or drainage conditions improve.

The calculator also reports a critical angle corresponding to FOS = 1.3. That is a useful screening number because it shows the steepest slope angle that still achieves a commonly referenced minimum planning threshold in many operating contexts. The value should be treated as an indicative limit, not a final design angle, because real mine slopes are controlled by structure, groundwater, scale effects, and bench configuration that this simplified method does not represent.

FOS Standards Reference Table

Different projects and guidance documents use different target safety factors depending on risk tolerance, operating life, uncertainty, and consequence of failure. In practice, open pit slopes are often categorized into broad bands so engineers and planners can quickly see whether a geometry is clearly unsafe, borderline, or likely acceptable for further evaluation. The table below is a practical screening reference based on common geotechnical practice and the general approach reflected in guidance from bodies such as SME and ICOLD, not a binding design code by itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is factor of safety in slope stability?

Factor of safety is the ratio of available shear resistance to the shear stress trying to cause movement. A value above 1.0 means resistance exceeds driving stress in the model, while a value below 1.0 indicates likely failure.

What FOS is required for mine slopes?

There is no single universal value. Many preliminary mining assessments use about 1.3 as a minimum screening target for operational slopes, but the required value depends on uncertainty, groundwater, scale, failure consequence, and company or regulator standards.

How does pore pressure affect stability?

Pore pressure reduces effective normal stress along the potential failure plane. Because frictional resistance depends on effective normal stress, higher pore pressure lowers resistance and pushes factor of safety downward.

Is this calculator suitable for design?

No. This tool is for education and preliminary screening only. Final mine slope design should use site investigation data, structural interpretation, groundwater modeling, and rigorous limit-equilibrium or numerical analysis.

What causes pit slope failure?

Typical causes include steep geometry, weak or weathered material, adverse joints or bedding, high pore pressure, poor drainage, toe erosion, blast damage, and seismic or operational disturbance. Failures often result from several factors acting together rather than a single cause.
